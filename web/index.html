revalign-teardown public FCC records

The internal photos hardware makers file before you can buy the product.

Every device with a radio has to pass FCC certification before it can be sold in the US, and the filing includes internal photographs of the board. Makers can hide those photos for up to 180 days — a clock that auto-expires. Type a brand and you get a wall of them. Every card links to the live FCC record; the FCC ID is printed on the device itself.

These are public compliance records, not leaks — the FCC releases each internal photo only after the maker's own confidentiality window expires. Read the FCC ID off the back of any device, look it up on fcc.gov, and you'll see the same photos. A red badge means granted recently — a signal the product is close, not a confirmed unreleased device.

revalign-teardown is an open-source demo by RevAlign. Data comes from the FCC Office of Engineering & Technology Equipment Authorization System (fcc.gov), which is public by law under 47 CFR Part 2. We are not the source of record; every card is a link to the FCC's. Grant dates approximate a product's readiness — they are not launch dates, and "recent" is a timing signal, not a claim about any specific unreleased product. We anchor on brands whose internal photos actually go public; some makers keep theirs confidential indefinitely.
